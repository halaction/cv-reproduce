The quantitative ordering is clear in the exported results. ConvMixer reaches the best logged test accuracy of \convmixeracc, ResNet18 reaches \resnetacc, and DeiT-Tiny reaches only \deitacc. This means ConvMixer is ahead of ResNet18 by roughly four percentage points and ahead of DeiT-Tiny by almost twenty-five points in absolute test accuracy. Even in this compact CIFAR-10 setting, the ConvMixer design remains highly competitive.

Runtime behavior tells a different story. ConvMixer requires about \convmixertrainsec~seconds per training epoch, compared with \resnettrainsec~seconds for ResNet18 and \deittrainsec~seconds for DeiT-Tiny. Evaluation follows the same pattern: ConvMixer takes \convmixerevalsec~seconds per epoch, compared with \resnetevalsec~seconds for ResNet18 and \deitevalsec~seconds for DeiT-Tiny. This is notable because ConvMixer has only \convmixerparams{} trainable parameters, far fewer than ResNet18 (\resnetparams{}) and also fewer than DeiT-Tiny (\deitparams{}), yet it is still the slowest model in wall-clock terms in the current implementation.

The result pattern is also consistent with the intended interpretation of the benchmark. ResNet18 benefits from a strong convolutional prior and remains a reliable baseline on small natural-image datasets, reaching \resnetacc{} despite being a conventional architecture. DeiT-Tiny, in contrast, is much weaker under the present recipe, which suggests that this shared small-scale training configuration is less favorable to the transformer-style baseline. ConvMixer occupies an interesting middle ground: it keeps explicit convolutional mixing while adopting patch-based processing, and in this reproduction that combination appears to be the most effective among the tested options.

This comparison should still be interpreted with care. The exported main runs did not stop at the same epoch: ConvMixer stopped at epoch \convmixerepochs, ResNet18 at epoch \resnetepochs, and DeiT-Tiny at epoch \deitepochs. In addition, the corresponding run states in the export are marked as killed rather than cleanly finished. The ranking is therefore meaningful as an observation from one shared codebase and logging setup, but it is not yet a fully controlled apples-to-apples benchmark with matched training budgets.
